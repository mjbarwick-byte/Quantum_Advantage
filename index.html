United States Likely to Maintain Quantum Sensing Lead Through 2035 — But Only Narrowly

Quantum advantage is likely (55–80%) to go to the actor that commits to the promising technologies, avoids the implausible ones, and best integrates the complete quantum ecosystem across workforce, manufacturing, data fusion, and fielding pipelines. The competition is shifting from a traditional measure-countermeasure cycle toward an asymmetrical race in which advantage accrues to whoever fields operational capability first.

1

United States Likely to Maintain Quantum Sensing Lead Through 2035 as PRC Generates Localized Surprise in Specific Mission Areas

By 2035, the US–PRC quantum sensing competition is likely to shift from a measure-countermeasure cycle toward an asymmetrical acquisition race, in which advantage accrues to the actor that first fields operational capability rather than the actor with superior laboratory performance.

The United States retains the broader quantum ecosystem advantage, including ten times higher private capital investment, a deeper academic base, and a mature defense laboratory network. The PRC's civil-military fusion model partially offsets this aggregate disadvantage by directly channeling university graduates into military quantum roles, bypassing the 6–18 month security clearance timelines that bleed the US defense talent pool.

"The actor that fields first is therefore likely to retain advantage for the duration of this gap, reinforcing deployment-driven dynamics that shape the vulnerability window."

PRC quantum communications investment — greater than its direct quantum sensing investment — is likely to generate technology spillover. Single-photon detectors, atomic clock technology supporting BeiDou, and laser-cooling expertise at USTC and the Chinese Academy of Sciences are likely to translate into sensing applications. The $359 million open-source figure for direct PRC quantum sensing investment likely understates effective PRC sensing capacity.

Allied quantum cooperation, particularly through AUKUS Pillar II, is likely to accelerate specific US fielding timelines. Australia trialed quantum clocks in November 2025; the UK completed the first test flight of a quantum navigation system. AUKUS export control alignment, finalized August 2024, has reduced ITAR/EAR constraints on quantum sensing component movement between allied foundries.

PRC Dual-Use Quantum Sensing at Belt and Road Facilities Likely to Evade Standard Indications and Warning by 2035

PRC dual-use quantum sensing at BRI facilities is likely to produce strategic surprise by 2035 by operating outside the indications-and-warning frameworks the Joint Force uses to detect adversary capability development. The mechanism of surprise is not sensor performance, but deployment under civilian attribution.

Over 40 PRC-operated port facilities at strategic chokepoints — including Djibouti, Gwadar, and Hambantota — combined with undersea cable landing stations and satellite ground stations across four continents, provide the stable, powered environments required for quantum sensing systems.

"PRC sensing capability is likely to persist undetected within commercial environments, producing collection blindness that is itself a source of strategic surprise distinct from the underlying sensor physics."

COSCO's Chancay port in Peru, operational since November 2024, establishes the first PRC-controlled deep-water facility in South America, while 22 BRI agreements across Latin America and the Caribbean extend potential sensing coverage beyond the Indo-Pacific into SOUTHCOM.

Three Joint Force gaps are likely to compound simultaneously. First, there is no established monitoring framework for PRC dual-use quantum deployment under commercial cover. Second, platform and doctrinal adaptation timelines average over a decade. Third, CJADC2 data integration remains unresolved, with GAO documenting $1.4 billion in investment over six years without achieving a cross-service data-sharing architecture.

Ecosystem Integration, Not Laboratory Sensitivity, Likely to Decide Quantum Advantage by 2035

Through 2035, one enabling material pathway and four integration barriers are likely to determine whether laboratory quantum sensing translates into fielded military advantage. The enabling pathway is CMOS manufacturing compatibility — the single material condition separating fieldable quantum sensing from laboratory demonstration.

The first barrier is environmental ruggedization. Vibration, thermal drift, and electromagnetic interference are likely to confine quantum sensing deployment to stable, fixed sites and strategic platforms through 2035. DARPA's RoQS program is likely to advance ruggedization standards but is unlikely to deliver sufficient miniaturization for tactical platforms.

"These four barriers do not operate independently — they compound. Partial progress across barriers is therefore unlikely (20–45%) to produce operational advantage."

The CJADC2 data fusion gap is the most consequential barrier because it is likely to strand otherwise successful sensor programs at the platform level. GAO documented in April 2025 that the Department of War spent $1.4 billion over six years on CJADC2 without producing a framework for sharing data across any sensor modality.

The workforce pipeline deficit compounds the other barriers: the absence of a dedicated DoW quantum sensing career field, combined with clearance delays and competition with private industry, is likely to constrain US fielding timelines. Training pipelines for strategic platforms are likely sufficient, but there is no pathway for tactical-level operators.
